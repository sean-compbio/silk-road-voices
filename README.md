# Silk Road Voices

*Recovering the Sogdian Ancient Letters for a wider audience*

## The Discovery

In 1907, the British-Hungarian archaeologist Aurel Stein was surveying a series of ruined watchtowers along the ancient Chinese frontier, at the edge of the Taklamakan desert in what is now western China. Inside one of them, hidden in a crevice in the wall, he found a weathered leather mailbag, its surface dried and stiffened by centuries without care. Opening it must have required deliberate hands. Inside were letters, their paper yellowed by time but largely intact, the ink still legible. They were written in a script none of Stein's team could immediately read, in a language that had not been spoken for centuries.

The letters were dated to around 313 AD. They had survived the harsh desert climate for nearly sixteen hundred years, preserved by the very aridity that made the Silk Road so dangerous to travel. Stein and his colleagues carefully catalogued what they had found, recognizing that the contents were extraordinary even before they could be deciphered. It would take decades of scholarship before the language was conclusively identified as Sogdian, one of the great trading languages of the ancient Silk Road, spoken across a vast network that once stretched from China to the Byzantine frontier.

The archaeologists surmised that the letters were meant to be delivered westward to Sogdia, the merchants' homeland in what is now Uzbekistan. They never arrived. No one knows why. The mailbag was sealed in the watchtower wall and remained there, unread, for seventeen centuries.

## The Letters

Among the oldest surviving personal correspondence in any Iranian language, the letters contain stories, thoughts and observations written by merchants, traders, and at least one stranded woman writing to a husband who had disappeared. They describe business arrangements, family struggles, money owed and cloth unsold. One letter records the fate of an orphan girl left in the care of a priest. Another contains instructions about interest payments on a deposit. The concerns are ordinary, human, and specific.

And embedded in those ordinary concerns, almost incidentally, one letter records the collapse of a dynasty. "Luoyang is no more. Ye is no more." Luoyang and Ye were ancient Chinese cities and seats of imperial power. History already knew they fell. What the Sogdian letters add is something rarer: a timestamp and a witness. These are words written by people who were there, watching it happen, not yet knowing that what they were witnessing would be remembered as the end of an era.

The authors intended their letters to reach friends, family, and business partners in Sogdia, their homeland thousands of miles to the west. A courier would have carried the mailbag along the Silk Road, across some of the most dangerous terrain in the ancient world. But for reasons that scholars still debate, the letters never left the watchtower. Perhaps the courier was caught in the very collapse the letters describe. Perhaps the Chinese frontier authorities confiscated them. Perhaps the messenger simply perished on the road, and the bag was left in the wall by someone who saw no value in words they could not read. Whatever happened, the letters waited in silence, sealed in stone, for seventeen centuries.

Their authors were not historians. They were not trying to preserve a record for the future. They were writing home.

## About This Project

This is not a retranslation or an academic publication. The primary translations are the work of specialists, credited in full, whose decades of scholarship made the original texts legible again. The project adds a curatorial layer: historical context, editorial framing, and illustration, assembled so that a reader encountering these texts for the first time can understand not just what the words say, but why they matter and who wrote them.
